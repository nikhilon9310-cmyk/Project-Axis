01
Origin Story & Problem Statement
How everyday observations led to a novel engineering concept
Problem ▾

Every engineering solution begins with a real observation. This section documents the chain of observations that produced AXIS — because the thinking process is as important as the design itself.

🛺
Observation 01 · Auto Rickshaw
Noticed the 3-wheel configuration on the way to coaching — 1 front wheel, 2 rear wheels. Minimal wheels, maximum stability.
→ Solved the wheel geometry problem for the robot's legs
🛼
Observation 02 · Roller Skates
Recognized that inline skate geometry offers low energy consumption and narrow footprint — perfect for a robot operating in human environments.
→ Inspired the hybrid leg + wheel locomotion concept
✈️
Observation 03 · Fighter Jet Missile Ejection
Observed how jets don't just release missiles — they push them away from the body first to prevent collision. Two-stage: release + eject.
→ Directly applied to the autonomous battery ejection mechanism
🎯
The Core Problem
Current robotic systems are single-mode — wheeled robots fail on rough terrain, bipedal robots are energy-inefficient and hard to balance, aerial drones can't operate at ground level. No single system handles all three environments autonomously.
💡
The Solution Hypothesis
A modular robot that combines hybrid ground locomotion (legs + wheels) with a detachable aerial unit — switching modes based on real-time terrain sensing — can operate continuously across all terrain types.
🎬
Target Application: Film Production
Directors like SS Rajamouli who prioritize real-world shooting over green screen face enormous camera crew costs — cameramen, rigs, insurance, permits. AXIS consolidates ground + aerial cinematography into one autonomous system, reducing crew cost and unlocking new camera angles physically impossible with human operators.
02
Wheel Configuration — Decision Analysis
Three options evaluated, one selected with full reasoning
Analysis ▾

The wheel geometry of each leg went through three iterations before arriving at the final design. Each rejection was based on specific engineering reasoning.

Option A
4-wheel car style (2×2)
High energy consumption. Wide footprint defeats the space advantage of using legs instead of wheels. Defeats the entire purpose of the bipedal design.
Rejected
Option B
Inline (4 wheels, single line)
Energy efficient and narrow — but severe sideways balance problem. Robot tips laterally with any lateral force. Unsafe for a heavy system.
Rejected
Option C ✓
Tricycle (1 front + 2 rear)
Inspired by auto-rickshaw geometry. Minimum wheels for maximum stability. Front wheel enables tight turning. Two rear wheels prevent sideways tipping. More energy efficient than Option A. More stable than Option B.
Selected
Configuration Stability Energy Use Footprint Turning
4-wheel (2×2) High High Wide Large radius
Inline (4 wheels) Low Low Narrow Small radius
Tricycle (1+2) ✓ Good Moderate Narrow Small radius
Passive Efficiency Bonus

The tricycle configuration causes a natural forward lean during movement — identical to how auto-rickshaws lean into motion. This lean assists forward momentum passively, reducing the energy the drive motors need to expend. A free efficiency gain from geometry alone.

Stair Climbing Mode

When the terrain sensor detects stairs or rough terrain ahead, all three wheels on each leg lock rigid. The leg joints take over, lifting the robot over the obstacle step by step. Wheel material uses vulcanized rubber (same as vehicle tires) to prevent slipping on edges during the transition.

03
Structural & Mechanical Design
Body architecture, materials, and center-of-mass engineering
Design ▾

Every structural decision in AXIS is driven by one principle: keep the center of mass low and the processing centralized to minimize wiring complexity.

🦴
Leg Architecture
Bipedal leg structure with hip, knee, and ankle joints — mirroring human anatomy for maximum terrain adaptability. Each leg terminates in the tricycle wheel assembly (1 front toe wheel + 2 rear heel wheels). Joint actuation via servo motors in Phase 1; upgrade to brushless motors in Phase 2.
⚖️
Center of Mass Strategy
Batteries placed in hips and lower torso — the heaviest components sit as low as possible. Processing units placed in upper chest — centralized to minimize wiring to all limbs and sensors. Head/drone contains only its own dedicated systems. Result: low, stable CoM that resists tipping.
🧱
Materials — Phased Approach
Phase 1 (Prototype): Aluminum frame + ABS plastic panels. Reduces cost, sufficient strength for testing. Phase 2 (Production): Carbon fiber elements added progressively — starting with the drone head (most weight-sensitive component) and moving to limbs. Carbon fiber is 40% lighter than aluminum at comparable strength.
🧎
Kneeling Stabilization Protocol
Before drone detachment, the body executes a kneeling sequence — lowering CoM, widening stance, and pre-absorbing the liftoff thrust. This is dynamic stability compensation: the robot preemptively adjusts for the weight loss and upward force of drone liftoff rather than reacting to it. Inspired independently; used in military aviation and spacecraft docking systems.
Engineering Honesty — Known Limitation

Bipedal locomotion is inherently less energy-efficient than wheeled movement on flat terrain. The wheel-assist mode mitigates this significantly, but the robot will always consume more power than a pure wheeled system on smooth surfaces. This trade-off is intentional — the gain in terrain adaptability justifies the cost.

04
Detachable Drone Head System
Proactive terrain adaptation via modular aerial unit
Design ▾

The drone head is the most original component of AXIS. Unlike systems that react to terrain problems, AXIS anticipates them — and escapes vertically before getting stuck.

📷
Primary Function: Cinematic Camera
The drone head carries the main cinema-grade camera. During ground operation it records from a human eye-level perspective. On detachment it transitions to aerial cinematography — all within one continuous shot with no camera swap. This is physically impossible with current film equipment.
📡
Terrain Anticipation System
Chest-mounted camera continuously scans forward terrain. On-board processing classifies terrain type in real time. When unsuitable terrain is detected ahead — crowds, water, rubble, tight corridors — the detachment sequence initiates proactively, before the body reaches the obstacle. This is called proactive terrain adaptation.
🔋
Drone Self-Sufficiency
The drone head is semi-independent: own processor, own battery, own sensors, own propulsion. During docked operation it draws supplemental power from the body. On detachment it operates fully autonomously for approximately 10 minutes before returning for battery swap.
🤖
Body Behavior During Separation
When the drone detaches, the main body does not stop — it enters recovery mode. Using the chest camera for navigation, the body independently finds a path around the obstacle to reunite with the drone on the other side. The system operates as two coordinated robots briefly, then rejoins.
Why This is Novel

Current robotic systems react to terrain — they struggle through obstacles or stop. AXIS anticipates terrain and escapes vertically before encountering it. The concept of splitting into two independent units that later reunite is not present in any current consumer or industrial robot. This is the core intellectual contribution of this design.

05
Power Architecture & Energy Management
Dual power system with opportunistic self-charging
Power ▾

AXIS uses a dual-chemistry power system — two different battery technologies for two different types of energy demand — managed by an intelligent energy controller.

Power SourceTypeUsed ForCharacteristic
Primary Li-ion batteries Normal locomotion, camera, sensors High energy density, slow discharge
Burst Supercapacitors Stair climbing, drone liftoff, sudden balance correction Instant high power, charges in seconds
Drone Dedicated Li-ion Drone flight, camera, propulsion ~10 min flight, auto-swap on return
⏱️
Revised Battery Life Estimate: ~40 Minutes
Roller-assist locomotion significantly reduces motor load vs. pure bipedal walking. Battery in hips + lower torso provides large cell volume. Multiple tasks running simultaneously (terrain scanning, motor control, camera feed, balance correction) are offset by the energy-efficient wheel mode during flat terrain traversal.
🔌
Opportunistic Self-Charging
During idle periods — between takes on a film set, waiting for scene setup — AXIS automatically connects to a charging point and begins recharging. This mirrors the behavior of Amazon Kiva warehouse robots, which independently return to charging stations during low-demand periods. Independently arrived at the same solution.
06
Autonomous Battery Hot-Swap Mechanism
Fighter jet-inspired ejection system for zero-downtime drone operation
Mechanism ▾

The battery hot-swap system solves the drone's 10-minute flight limit without any human intervention. Inspired directly by fighter jet missile ejection mechanisms observed independently.

1
Low Battery Detection
Drone's onboard system monitors battery level continuously. At ~20% charge, an autonomous return signal is triggered. The drone completes its current movement, then navigates back to dock with the main body.
2
Drone Docking
Drone returns and re-docks on the body. Magnetic alignment guides the drone to the exact docking position. Electrical contacts re-establish connection. Body acknowledges dock — swap sequence initiates.
3
Stage 1 — Electromagnetic Release
Electromagnetic clamps holding the dead battery release simultaneously. Electrical contacts disconnect. Battery is now held only by the ejection mechanism — not by any active force.
4
Stage 2 — Spring-Loaded Directional Ejection
Inspired by fighter jet missile ejection: a spring-loaded ejector pushes the dead battery downward and outward in a controlled direction — ensuring it clears all internal components cleanly before falling into a collection tray in the lower torso. Two-stage process prevents jamming.
5
Stage 3 — Fresh Battery Loads
Next pre-charged battery advances on an internal rail system into the now-empty slot. Spring-loaded anode/cathode contacts click into place on contact. Electrical connection confirmed. Drone ready to detach within seconds.
6
Dead Battery Charges While Drone Flies
The ejected battery in the collection tray is connected to the body's charging circuit and begins recharging immediately. By the time the drone's fresh battery runs low, the previously dead battery is recharged and ready — creating a continuous loop with zero manual intervention.
Calibration Challenge — Acknowledged

Spring ejection force must be precisely calibrated: too weak and the battery fails to clear the mechanism; too strong and it risks damaging internal components. This is a key engineering challenge for the physical prototype phase, requiring iterative testing and adjustment of spring tension.

Cross-Domain Insight

This mechanism was derived by observing fighter jet missile ejection in a YouTube documentary and applying the two-stage release-then-push logic to the battery swap problem. This type of cross-domain engineering — solving one field's problem with another field's solution — is how most breakthrough inventions occur.

This is exactly why top university labs are the target.
