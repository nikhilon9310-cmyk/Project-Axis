About

Who I Am

I'm a Class XI student from India, preparing for JEE while building things on the side that actually interest me. I learn best by observing the world and asking why.

A robotics idea started from watching an auto-rickshaw on the way to coaching. A civic initiative started from noticing a dirty park. That's how my brain works — connections between everyday things and bigger problems.

I believe technology should amplify human potential, not replace it. That belief shapes everything I build and everything I write.

The Cupcake Wrapper

A small act of carelessness and a very large question about who we are becoming.

begins here

It was 7:30 in the evening. Coaching had just ended and everyone was spilling out onto the road — tired, hungry, phone in hand. I was one of them. And so was he.

He was walking ahead of me, loud on his phone, clearly in an argument with someone. In his other hand — a cupcake. He ate it in a few bites, still talking, still walking. And then, without a pause, without a glance, he let the wrapper fall. Just like that. Onto the road. And kept walking.

It lasted maybe two seconds. He probably doesn't remember it. But I haven't stopped thinking about it since.

"These are the future of our nation. In twenty years, they will be in offices, in Parliament, in boardrooms. And they just threw a wrapper on the road without blinking."

I want to be clear — I am not writing this to judge one person. I don't know him. He might be brilliant. He might be kind to his family. One wrapper doesn't define a human being. But it does reveal something. And that something is what I can't stop thinking about.

It's Not a Moral Problem. It's a Sensitivity Problem.

We talk about civic sense as if it's about morality. As if people litter because they are bad people. I don't think that's true. I think people litter because they have stopped feeling connected to the space around them.

The road doesn't feel like their road. The park doesn't feel like their park. Public space has become a place that belongs to nobody — and what belongs to nobody, nobody takes care of.

I tested this once. In the park near my home, I noticed people were littering consistently. Not because they were careless people — but because there was only one dustbin at one end of the park. Walking all the way there felt like too much effort. The environment was designed for littering, not against it.

So I put up a simple sign. Not a lecture about morality. Just a notice that said littering would be fined five hundred rupees. And it worked. The park got cleaner — not because people suddenly became better humans, but because the environment changed.

This is the real lesson: People respond to systems, not sermons. If you want behaviour to change, change the environment first. Moral lectures have never cleaned a single road.

But the park experiment only solves the symptom. The deeper question is — why do we need a fine to care about our own surroundings in the first place?

The Generation That Will Inherit Everything

Here is what unsettles me most. The person with the cupcake wrapper — he is my generation. Gen Z. The generation that posts about climate change, shares aesthetic reels about minimalism, and talks about mental health awareness.

We are, by every measure, the most informed generation in Indian history. We have the internet. We have access to every idea ever written. We know about pollution. We know about civic responsibility. We have seen the videos of overflowing landfills and choked rivers.

And we still throw the wrapper.

"Knowing and feeling are not the same thing. We know everything. We feel very little."

In twenty to thirty years, this generation will be everywhere — in government, in business, in the armed forces. The people making decisions about our cities, our infrastructure, our public spaces — they are currently walking out of coaching classes and dropping wrappers on the road.

I am not being pessimistic. I am being honest. And I think honesty is more useful than comfort right now.

What Can Actually Change This?

I have thought about this a lot. And I don't think the answer is more awareness campaigns or stricter laws — though those help. I think the answer is sensitivity.

Sensitivity to your surroundings. The feeling that this road, this park, this city — it is yours. Not the government's. Not someone else's. Yours. And you are responsible for it.

That feeling doesn't come from a poster. It comes from being raised to notice. To look around. To ask — what does this place look like because of me?

Most of us were never asked that question. Maybe that is where it starts. Not with fines. Not with campaigns. But with a simple habit of noticing — and caring about what you notice.

I am still working out what to do with that answer. But I think asking the question out loud is a start.
